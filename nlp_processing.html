Probabilities and Bayes' Rule¶

  • Conditional probabilities are defined in terms of unconditional probabilities of events or propositions $a$ and $b$ as follows:
$$ P(a\, \vert\, b) = \frac{P(a \land b)}{P(b)} $$
  • The above equation can also be written in the form of the product rule:
$$ P(a \land b) = P(a\, \vert\, b)P(b) $$
  • When two propositions or events $a$ and $b$ are independent, we have:
$$ P(a\, \vert\, b) = P(a)$$$$ P(b\, \vert\, a) = P(b)$$$$ P(a \land b) = P(a)P(b)$$
  • When we are talking about variables, not just events or propositions, we similarly have:
$$ P(X\, \vert\, Y) = P(X)$$$$ P(Y\, \vert\, X) = P(Y)$$$$ P(X, Y) = P(X)P(Y)$$

  • The product rule can actually be written in two different ways: $$ P(a \land b) = P(a\, \vert\, b)P(b) $$ and $$ P(a \land b) = P(b\, \vert\, a)P(a) $$

  • So combining the two forms we get: $$ P(b\, \vert\, a) = \frac{P(a\, \vert\, b)P(b)}{P(a)} $$ which is Bayes' rule (or Bayes' law, or Bayes' theorem).

  • Equivalently we have for variables:

$$ P(Y\, \vert\, X) = \frac{P(X\, \vert\, Y)P(Y)}{P(X)} $$

  • Baye's rule allows us to move from the causal direction to the diagnostic direction: $$ P(\textrm{cause}\, \vert\, \textrm{effect}) = \frac{P(\textrm{effect}\, \vert\, \textrm{cause}) P(\textrm{cause})}{P(\textrm{effect})} $$

  • $P(\textrm{effect}\, \vert\, \textrm{cause})$ describes the causal direction.

  • $P(\textrm{cause}\, \vert\, \textrm{effect})$ describes the diagnostic direction.

  • In a medical setting, $$ P(\textrm{symptoms}\, \vert\, \textrm{disease})$$ gives the probability that symptoms arise from a certain disease; that is the causal direction.

  • The doctor then wants to determine $$ P(\textrm{disease}\, \vert\, \textrm{symptoms}) $$ which is the probability that the patient suffers from a disease if the particular symptoms are exhibited.

  • For example, a doctor knows that the disease meningitis causes the patient to have a stiff neck, say, 70% of the time.

  • The doctor also knows some unconditional facts: the prior probability that a patient has meningitis is 1/50,000, and the prior probability that any patient has a stiff neck is 1%.

  • Letting $s$ be the proposition that the patient has a stiff neck and $m$ be the proposition that the patient has meningitis, we have:

$$P(s\, \vert\, m) = 0.7$$$$P(m) = 1/50000$$$$P(s) = 0.01$$$$P(m\, \vert\, s) = \frac{P(s\, \vert\, m)P(m)}{P(s)} = \frac{0.7 \times 1/50000}{0.01} = 0.0014$$

Naive Bayes Classification¶

  • Suppose that we have a problem instance to be classified in one of $k$ classes, $C_k$, based on $x_1$, $x_2$, $\ldots$, $x_n$ features.

  • That is equivalent to finding the maximum probability of: $$P(C_k\, \vert\, x_1, \dots, x_n)$$

  • Applying Bayes rule we get:

$$P(C_k\, \vert\, x_1, \dots, x_n) = \frac{P(C_k) \ P(x_1, \dots, x_n\, \vert\, C_k)}{P(x_1, \dots, x_n)}$$
  • Now, the denominator does not depend on $C_k$, so we can discard it in our quest to maximise the probability.
  • Recall the chain rule of probability:
$$P(A_n, \ldots , A_1) = P(A_n\,\vert\, A_{n-1}, \ldots , A_1) \cdot P( A_{n-1}, \ldots , A_1)$$
  • That means that the numerator is: $$P(C_k, x_1, \ldots, x_n)$$
  • And by repeated application of the chain rule we get:
\begin{align} P(C_k, x_1, \dots, x_n) & = P(x_n \,\vert\, x_1, \dots, x_{n-1}, C_k)\ P( x_1, \dots, x_{n-1}, C_k)\\ & = P(x_n \,\vert\, x_1, \dots, x_{n-1}, C_k)\ P( x_{n-1} \,\vert\, x_1 \dots, x_{n-2}, C_k)\ P( x_1, \dots, x_{n-2}, C_k)\\ & = P(x_n \,\vert\, x_1, \dots, x_{n-1}, C_k)\ \dots \ P(x_2 \,\vert\, x_1, C_k) \ P(x_1, C_k) \\ & = P(x_n \,\vert\, x_1, \dots, x_{n-1}, C_k)\ \dots \ P(x_2 \,\vert\, x_1, C_k) \ P(x_1 \,\vert\, C_k)\ P(C_k) \\ & = P(C_k) \ P(x_1 \,\vert\, C_k) \ P(x_2 \,\vert\, C_k, x_1) \ \dots\ P(x_n \,\vert\, C_k, x_1, x_2, x_3, \dots, x_{n-1}) \end{align}

  • This is where the "naive" part of the model comes into play.

  • We assume that each of the $x_1$, $x_2$, $\ldots$, $x_n$ features is independent of all the others.

  • So:

\begin{align} P(x_2 \,\vert\, C_k,x_1) & = P(x_2 \,\vert\, C_k)\\ P(x_3 \,\vert\, C_k,x_1, x_2) & = P(x_3\,\vert\, C_k)\\ \dots\\ P(x_n \,\vert\, C_k,x_1, \dots, x_{n-1}) & = P(x_n\,\vert\, C_k)\\ \end{align}
  • This means that we have:
$$P(C_k\, \vert\, x_1, \dots, x_n) \propto P(C_k)\ P(x_1 | C_k) \dots\ P(x_n | C_k)$$
  • Or:
$$ P(C_k\, \vert\, x_1, \dots, x_n) \propto P(C_k) \prod_{i = 1}^{n}{P(x_i \,\vert\, C_k)}$$
  • We can use that to build a classifier that assigns as class to an instance. In particular, the classifier will choose the class that maximises the value of the above function. $$ \underset{k \in \{1, \dots, K\}}{\operatorname{argmax}} \ P(C_k) \displaystyle\prod_{i=1}^n P(x_i \,\vert\, C_k)$$
  • To find the classifier we need to know the distribution of the probabilities $P(x_i \,\vert\, C_k)$.
  • If $x = (x_1, \dots, x_n)$, and $P(x \,\vert\, C_k)$ follows the multinomial distribution, then the classifier we use is the multinomial naive Bayes classifier.

Bernoulli, Binomial, Multinomial¶

  • But what exactly is the multinomial distribution?

  • It's worth spending some time to explore it, starting from the basics.

Bernoulli¶

  • If $X$ is a random variable that can take the value 1 with probability $p$ and the value 0 with probability $q = 1 - p$, then $X$ follows the Bernoulli distribution:
$$\Pr(X=1) = 1 - \Pr(X=0) = 1 - q = p$$
  • The probability mass function of the Bernoulli distribution is:
$$f(x;p)={ \begin{cases} p & {\text{if }}x=1, \\[6pt]1-p & {\text{if }}x=0. \end{cases} }$$
  • This can also be expressed as:
$$f(x;p)=p^{x}(1-p)^{1-x}\!\quad {\text{for }}x\in \{0,1\}$$

Binomial¶

  • If we have $n$ independent random variables, where each one of them can take the value 1 (success) with probability $p$ and the value 0 (failure) with probability $q = 1 - p$, then the probability that we will have $k$ successes is given by the Binomial distribution:
$$f(k;n,p)=\Pr(X=k)={\binom {n}{k}}p^{k}(1-p)^{n-k}$$

  • This is equivalent to the probability that we will get $k$ successes in $n$ trials, where each trial is independent of the others and follows the Bernoulli distribution.

  • So, the Bernoulli distribution is equal to the Binomial distribution for $n = 1$.

Multinomial¶

  • If we have $n$ independent random variables, where each one of them can take a value from $1, 2, \ldots, k$ with probability $p_k$, then the probability that we will get $x_1$ variables with value 1, $x_2$ variables with value 2, $\ldots$, $x_k$ variables with value $k$:
$$ \begin{aligned} f(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{k};n,p_{1},\ldots ,p_{k})&{}=\Pr(X_{1}=x_{1}{\text{ and }} X_{2} = x_{2}{\text{ and }} \dots {\text{ and }}X_{k}=x_{k})\\ &{}= \frac{n!}{x_{1}x_{2}!\cdots x_{k}!}p_{1}^{x_{1}}\cdots p_{k}^{x_{k}}\\ &{}= \binom{n!}{x_{1}, x{_2}, \ldots x_{k}}p_{1}^{x_{1}}\cdots p_{k}^{x_{k}} \end{aligned}$$
  • The expression: $$\binom{n!}{x_{1}, x{_2}, \ldots, x_{k}}$$ is the multinomial coefficient. It is the number of ways to partition $n$ objects to $k$ groups of size $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_k$.

  • For example, if we roll one die six times, what is the probability that we will get 3 ones, 2 twos and 1 threes?

  • It will be:

$$ f(3, 2, 1, 0, 0, 0; 6, 1/6, 1/6, 1/6, 1/6, 1/6, 1/6) = \frac{6!}{3! 2! 1! 0! 0! 0!}(1/6)^6 = \frac{5}{3888}$$

The Bag-of-Words Model¶

  • In language processing, we often model documents using the bag-of-words model.

  • In this model, we use a bag, a.k.a a multiset, to represent each document.

  • The bag contains, for the document, the counts of words that occur in the document, disregarding order, syntax, and grammar.

  • As an example, suppose we have the following two documents.
(1) John likes to watch movies. Mary likes movies too.

(2) John also likes to watch football games.
  • Here is the list of the words that appear in these two documents.
[
    "John",
    "likes",
    "to",
    "watch",
    "movies",
    "also",
    "football",
    "games",
    "Mary",
    "too"
]
  • And this is the bag-of-words representation for the two documents.
(1) [1, 2, 1, 1, 2, 0, 0, 0, 1, 1]
(2) [1, 1, 1, 1, 0, 1, 1, 1, 0, 0]
  • In each list, item i corresponds to the ith word. The value of item i is the number of times it occurs in the particular document.
  • We can understand the bag-of-words representation as a matrix.
    • We assign a fixed integer id to each word occurring in any document of the training set (for instance by building a dictionary from words to integer indices).
    • For each document i, we count the number of occurrences of each word w and store it in a matrix X[i, j] as the value of feature j where j is the index of word w in the dictionary.

  • The bags of words representation implies that n_features is the number of distinct words in the corpus: this number is typically larger that 100,000.

  • If n_samples == 10000, storing X as a numpy array of type float32 would require 10000 x 100000 x 4 bytes = 4GB in RAM.

  • Fortunately, most values in X will be zeros since for a given document less than a couple thousands of distinct words will be used.

  • For this reason we say that bags of words are typically high-dimensional sparse datasets. We can save a lot of memory by only storing the non-zero parts of the feature vectors in memory.

  • For this reason, scikit-learn uses sparse matrices provided by scipy.sparse.

  • What we have been calling words are actually tokens in language processing parlance.

  • We need a mechanism to break our documents into tokens and count them.

  • This is the job of CountVectorizer.

  • The bag-of-words is a sparse matrix represented in CSR (= Compressed Sparse Row) format.

  • The CSR format means that we store the contents of the sparse matrix using three arrays.

    • The array data (called A in the literature) holds the actual values stored in the matrix.
    • The array indptr(called IA in the literature) has $m+1$ elements, where $m$ is the number of rows in the non-sparse matrix. indptr[i] gives the index of the first element of row i in data. That means that the elements of row i in data extend from indptr[i] to indptr[i+1] - 1.
    • The array indices (called JA in the literature) holds the column index of each element in A and is therefore the same size with A.

tf-idf¶

  • Occurrence counts is simple and makes for a good start, but there are problems when we are dealing with large documents.

  • Terms in large documents will display higher frequency counts, even if the documents talk about the same topics.

  • To get around this problem we can go beyond frequency counts and use the tf-idf metric.

  • The tf metric stands for term frequency.

  • The simplest way to calculate tf is simply as the raw frequency of a term in a document, i.e., the number of times that term t occurs in document d.

  • The idf metric stands for inverse document frequency.

  • The idf measures how much information a term provides, that is, how common is a term inside a document corpus. $$\mathrm{idf}(t, D) = \log \frac{N}{|\{d \in D: t \in d\}|}$$

  • In the above formula:

    • $D$ is the document corpus.
    • $N$ is the total number of documents in the corpus, $N = {|D|}$
    • $|\{d \in D: t \in d\}|$ : number of documents where the term $t$ appears (i.e., $\mathrm{tf}(t,d) \neq 0)$.

  • If a term does not appear in the corpus, this will lead to division by zero, so we often adjust it by adding one to the denominator: $$\mathrm{idf}(t, D) = \log \frac{N}{1 + |\{d \in D: t \in d\}|}$$

  • With idf we downscale weights for words that occur in many documents in the corpus and are therefore less informative than those that occur only in a smaller portion of the corpus.

  • We therefore arrive at the tf-idf metric: $$\textrm{tf-idf}(t,d,D) = \mathrm{tf}(t,d) \times \mathrm{idf}(t, D)$$

  • In fact, scikit-learn uses a slightly different variant: $$\textrm{tf-idf}(t,d,D) = \mathrm{tf}(t,d) \times (\mathrm{idf}(t, D) + 1) = \mathrm{tf} + \mathrm{tf} \times \mathrm{idf}$$

  • We add one so that terms with zero idf, i.e. that occur in all documents of a training set, will not be entirely ignored.

  • After we calculate the tf-idf, we want to make sure that documents with similar content look indeed similar, even though one may be much longer than the other.

  • Since the similarity between two documents is represented by the tf-idf vector for that document, we want to scale the vector down so that all documents have the same unit norm.

  • All these transformations are implemented by the TfidfTransformer class.

  • We mentioned that all feature vectors will have the same magnitude.

  • This is done by normalizing with the $\ell^2$ (Euclidean) norm.

  • If $x = (x_1, \dots, x_n)$ then: $$\left\| x \right\| = \sqrt{x_1^2 + \cdots + x_n^2}$$

  • The normalized vector is: $$\frac{x}{\left\| x \right\|}$$

  • We can also use the $\ell_1$ norm, defined as:
$$\left\| x \right\| _1 := \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left| x_i \right|$$
  • This is also known as Taxicab norm or Manhattan norm, because it relates to the distance a taxi has to drive in a rectangular street grid to get from the origin to the point $x$.
  • If we want to get the idf values computed for each feature, we can do so via the idf_ attribute of the transformer.

  • Let's also have a look at the classification report and the confusion matrix.

  • Remember that the classification report displays the following metrics:

    • precision
    • recall
    • $F_1$ score
    • support, which is the number of instances reported in each class

  • Precision is the ratio of true positives over all reported positives.

  • Equivalently, it is the number of correct positive results divided by the number of positive results.

  • Mathematically, it is: $$\text{precision} = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}$$

  • Or, specifically, in information retrieval: $$\text{precision} = \frac{|\text{relevant documents|}\cap \text{|retrieved documents|}}{|\text{retrieved documents}|}$$

  • Recall is the ratio of of true positives over all actual positives.

  • Equivalently, it is the ratio of true positives over the sum of true positives and false negatives.

  • Or, the number of correct positive results divided by the number of positive results that should have been returned.

  • Mathematically, it is: $$\text{recall} = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}$$

  • Or, specifically, in information retrieval: $$\text{recall} = \frac{|\text{relevant documents|}\cap \text{|retrieved documents|}}{|\text{relevant documents}|}$$

  • It is also called sensitivity.

  • We can understand it as the probability that a relevant document is retrieved.

  • The $F_1$ score takes both the precision and the recall into account.

  • Mathematically, it is the harmonic mean of the precision and the recall.

  • The harmonic mean of $n$ numbers $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n$ is defined as:

$$H={\frac {n}{{\frac {1}{x_{1}}}+{\frac {1}{x_{2}}}+\cdots +{\frac {1}{x_{n}}}}}$$
  • So, the $F_1$ score is:
$$F_{1}=2\cdot \frac{1}{\tfrac{1}{\mathrm{recall}}+\tfrac{1}{\mathrm{precision}}} = 2\cdot {\frac{\mathrm{precision} \cdot \mathrm{recall}}{\mathrm{precision} +\mathrm{recall}}}$$

Parameter Tuning Using Grid Search¶

  • There are many parameters that affect the performance of our models. For example:
    • use_idf in TfidfTransformer
    • MultinomialNB includes a smoothing parameter alpha
    • SGDClassifier has a penalty parameter alpha

  • Instead of tweaking the parameters of the various components of the chain, it is possible to run an exhaustive search of the best parameters on a grid of possible values.

  • We try out all classifiers on either words or bigrams, with or without idf, and with a penalty parameter of either 0.01 or 0.001 for the linear SVM.

  • We'll put the parameters of the search in a dictionary. Each key of the dictionary will be composed by the pipeline element followed by the parameter name. The values of each key will form the search space for the grid search.

Feature Hashing¶

  • The mapping from tokens to integers that we have been using is simple and works well in many cases.

  • However, we need to hold in memory the mapping from the string tokens to the integer feature indices.

  • This is what we saw with the vocabulary_ attribute.

  • This has problems with big datasets.

  • The larger the corpus, the larger the vocabulary will grow and hence the memory use too.

  • Building the word-mapping requires a full pass over the dataset hence it is not possible to fit text classifiers in a strictly online manner.

  • To get arround this problem, we can use the hashing trick.

  • In particular, we apply a hash function to the features and use their hash values as indices directly, rather than looking the indices up in an associative array.

  • To achieve that, we use the FeatureHasher class.

  • Instances of FeatureHasher apply a hash function to the features to determine their column index in sample matrices directly.

  • The result is increased speed and reduced memory usage, at the expense of inspectability; the hasher does not remember what the input features looked like and has no inverse_transform method.

In [56]:
def hashing_vectorizer(features, n):
    v = np.zeros(n, dtype=int)
    for f in features:
        h = hash(f)
        v[h % n] += 1
    return v

  • For instance, suppose a document has the following features: ['cat', 'dog', 'man', 'woman'].

  • And also suppose that we expect our total vocabulary to be of size 10.

  • Then the result of the hashing_vectorizer will be:

In [57]:
features = ['cat', 'dog', 'man', 'woman']
v = hashing_vectorizer(features, 10)
print(v)

[0 0 0 0 1 0 0 2 0 1]

  • Since the hash function might cause collisions between (unrelated) features, a signed hash function is used and the sign of the hash value determines the sign of the value stored in the output matrix for a feature.

  • This way, collisions are likely to cancel out rather than accumulate error, so that the hash function is unbiased.

  • If non_negative=True is passed to the constructor, the absolute value is taken.

  • This undoes some of the collision handling, but allows the output to be passed to estimators like MultinomialNB.

In [61]:
import mmh3

def hashing_vectorizer(features, n):
    v = np.zeros(n, dtype=int)
    for f in features:
        h = mmh3.hash(f)
        idx = h % n
        if h < 1 == 1:
            v[idx] += 1
        else:
            v[idx] -= 1
    return v

v = hashing_vectorizer(features, 10)
print(v)

  • Feature hashing can be employed in document classification, but unlike CountVectorizer, FeatureHasher does not do word splitting or any other preprocessing except Unicode-to-UTF-8 encoding.

  • So we either need to write our own tokenizer, or we can use HashingVectorizer, which is a combined tokenizer/hasher.

  • HashingVectorizer is stateless, so we do not need to call fit on it; we just call transform directly.

  • In practice collisions are rarely a problem, because the hash table is to the order of $2^{18}$, so collisions are likely to be rare.

  • The size of the hash table can be adjusted with the n_features parameter; a small n_features value can lead to collisions.

Latent Dirichlet Allocation¶

  • The Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) is a very popular method for identifying topics in documents (and not only).

  • It assumes that documents are generated through a probabilistic generative process.

  • To gain some insight inside the generative process, we need to understand the Dirichlet distribution (which gives the name to LDA). To do that, we should first have a look at the Beta distribution.

The Beta Distribution¶

  • We know that if we have a coin that comes up heads with probabity $p$, then the probability that we will have $k$ heads out of $n$ coin tosses is given by the Binomial distribution:
$$f(k;n,p)=\Pr(X=k)={\binom {n}{k}}p^{k}(1-p)^{n-k}$$

  • But suppose that we do not know exactly the probability $p$; suppose instead that we know, from prior history, that the coin had $a$ heads and $b$ tails.

  • Based on this prior knowledge we want to select a $p$ that is consistent with these prior results.

  • It turns out that a distribution that would fit our needs is of the form: $$f(x; a, b) = \text{constant}\cdot x^{a-1} (1-x)^{b-1}$$

  • The constant is neede to ensure that the total probability integrates to 1. So it is: $$ \text{constant} = \frac{1}{\int_{0}^{1}u^{a-1}(1-u)^{b-1}\mathrm{du}} $$

  • But then, by definition: $$ \int_{0}^{1}u^{a-1}(1-u)^{b-1}\mathrm{du} = \text{B}(a, b)$$

  • So we arrive at the Beta distribution: $$\text{Beta}(x; a, b) = \frac{1}{\text{B}(a, b)}x^{a-1} (1-x)^{b-1}$$

  • The mean of Beta is: $$\frac{a}{a+b}$$ and the variance is: $$\frac{ab}{(a + b)^2(a + b + 1)}$$

  • Note that: $$\text{B}(a, b) = \frac{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)}{\Gamma(a + b)}$$

  • So we have:

$$\text{Beta}(x; a, b) = \frac{\Gamma(a + b)}{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)}x^{a-1} (1-x)^{b-1}$$

  • And what is this mysterious $\Gamma(\cdot)$ function?

  • It is the generalisation of the factorial function to complex (and therefore real) numbers.

  • It is: $$\Gamma (z)=\int _{0}^{\infty }x^{z-1}e^{-x}\,\mathrm {d} x$$

  • From which it follows that for any natural number $n$: $$\Gamma(n) = (n - 1)!$$

The Dirichlet Distribution¶

  • The Dirichlet distribution is a multivariate extension of the Beta distribution.

  • Whereas the Beta distribution is a distribution over Binomial distributions, the Dirichlet distribution is a distribution over Multinomial distributions.

  • Mathematically, it is:
$$f\left(x_{1}, x_{2},\cdots ,x_{K};\alpha _{1}, \alpha_{2}, \cdots ,\alpha _{k}\right)={\frac {1}{\mathrm{B} (\alpha_{1}, \alpha_{2}, \cdots, \alpha_{k})}\prod _{i=1}^{K}x_{i}^{\alpha _{i}-1}}$$
  • where $\mathrm{B}(\alpha_{1}, \alpha_{2}, \cdots, \alpha_{k})$ is the multivariate Beta function:
$$\mathrm {B}(\alpha_{1}, \alpha_{2}, \cdots, \alpha_{k})={\frac {\prod _{i=1}^{K}\Gamma (\alpha _{i})}{\Gamma \left(\sum _{i=1}^{K}\alpha _{i}\right)}}$$
  • So:
$$f\left(x_{1}, x_{2},\cdots ,x_{K};\alpha _{1}, \alpha_{2}, \cdots ,\alpha _{k}\right)= {\frac{\Gamma \left(\sum _{i=1}^{K}\alpha _{i}\right)}{\prod _{i=1}^{K}\Gamma (\alpha _{i})}\prod _{i=1}^{K}x_{i}^{\alpha _{i}-1}}$$

The LDA Generative Process¶

  • To generate a document:
    • Randomly choose a distribution over topics. These will be the topics of the document. The distribution used to choose the distribution of the topic documents is a Dirichlet distribution.
    • For each word that will enter the document:
      • Randomly choose a topic from the distribution of topics of the document (this is a multinomial distribution).
      • Randomly choose a word from the distribution over words that makes up the topic (this is a multinomial distribution).
      • Add the word in the document.
